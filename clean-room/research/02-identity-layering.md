# R2 — Identity Layering
- Date: 2026-04-27
- Scope: How creators integrate religious / values / philosophical identity into professional personal sites without alienating audiences who don't share that identity. Christian-aware focus.
- Method: Live WebFetch of hero copy + WebSearch context. Hero text quoted verbatim; paraphrased text marked with brackets.

---

## A. Strategies (6)

### Strategy 1 — Faith-Forward Hero (overt theological hook above the fold)
The hero copy itself stakes a Christian claim. Visitor knows the worldview within one sentence. The site is built for an audience that wants this signal up front.

**Examples:**
- **Jordan Raynor — jordanraynor.com.** Hero: *"Your work matters for eternity."* Nav includes "Devotionals." Sub-CTA on page: *"Join 100,000+ Christians who receive The Word Before Work devotionals."* About page opens: *"Jordan Raynor is a leading voice of the faith and work movement."*
- **Russell Moore — russellmoore.com.** Tagline: *"Engaging the Culture without Losing the Gospel."* Featured article visible above fold treats Christian themes (John Perkins, evangelicalism) directly.
- **Skye Jethani — skyejethani.com → withgoddaily.com.** Hero: *"The devotional for people who hate devotionals."* Subhead: *"Daily devotionals written by Skye Jethani."* The personal domain redirects entirely to the devotional product — faith *is* the product.

**Why it works:** Self-selects audience instantly. No bait-and-switch. The Christian-curious / Christian-aligned visitor feels at home in 2 seconds.

**Why it can fail:** Closes the door on mixed-faith clients who don't yet know Fellipe. The hero doubles as a filter — that's the bet.

**Transferability to Fellipe:** Partial. Fellipe explicitly does *not* want to alienate non-Christian clients, so a Raynor-style "for eternity" hero overshoots. A *softer* faith-forward hook (a values phrase, not a theological claim) is more on-target.

---

### Strategy 2 — Layered Bridge Headline (faith-adjacent vocabulary, theology in nav/about)
Hero uses language that a Christian reads as theological and a non-Christian reads as values-based ("calling," "stewardship," "purpose," "for eternity is not used," "build something that lasts"). Explicit faith content lives one click deep.

**Examples:**
- **Andy Crouch — andy-crouch.com.** Hero: *"Andy Crouch"* / *"Author, musician, public speaker."* Featured book *The Life We're Looking For* talks about being *"recognized — to be seen, loved, and embedded in rich relationships."* Christian framing (Mammon, image-bearing, Praxis "redemptive entrepreneurship") shows up in book copy and bio, not the masthead.
- **Bob Goff — bobgoff.com.** Hero: *"Life's too short to just go through the motions."* Subhead: *"Guiding you to find & live the extraordinary life you always wanted."* Title: *"Author, Speaker, Coach and Dreamer."* The Christian frame appears as *"fueled by grace"* and a sub-section product *"A Journey With Jesus."* Faith is unmistakable but never the first word.

**Why it works:** Mixed audience can parse the hero on their own terms. The Christian visitor reads "grace" / "calling" theologically; the secular reader reads them as values. No one is excluded at the door.

**Why it can fail:** Christian-first audiences may not recognize the site as Christian quickly enough. Risk of looking like a generic productivity / lifestyle author.

**Transferability:** Strong. This is the closest match to Fellipe's stated goal.

---

### Strategy 3 — Credentialed Polymath Stack (faith as one identity layer among many)
Hero is a list of identities. Faith is one item — equally weighted with profession, family, hobbies. No item dominates.

**Examples:**
- **DHH — dhh.dk** (secular analog, but the structural pattern is the canonical version). Hero: *"I am the creator of Ruby on Rails, creator of Omarchy, co-owner of 37signals, best-selling author, Le Mans class-winning racing driver, investor in Danish startups, Shopify board member, frequent podcast guest, and family man."* Multiple identities, no hierarchy. A Christian creator could list "Christian" or "follower of Jesus" in this stack.
- **John Dyer — j.hn.** Hero: *"Coder and Professor, Technology and Faith."* Subtitle elsewhere: *"Theology, Technology, Society."* Two-domain identity (coder + Christian theologian) presented as compound, not subordinate.

**Why it works:** Reads as honest self-disclosure rather than evangelism. Christianity becomes a fact about the person, not a sales pitch. Doesn't alienate — visitors see a whole human and pick which thread to pull.

**Why it can fail:** Can feel like a LinkedIn salad if the stack is too long. Loses the rhetorical punch of a single claim.

**Transferability:** Very strong. Fellipe's actual identity (Christian, entrepreneur, founder, builder, writer) maps cleanly onto this. The stack itself signals that no one identity is hidden or apologized for.

---

### Strategy 4 — Faith-Coded Nav and Categories (hero neutral, taxonomy reveals)
Hero is professional/neutral. Faith surfaces through navigation labels, content categories, or tags rather than the masthead.

**Examples:**
- **Carey Nieuwhof — careynieuwhof.com.** Hero is generic ("growth"-themed). Nav exposes faith implicitly: *Church Growth*, *Communication and Preaching*, *Academy*. No "I am a Christian" sentence above the fold; the taxonomy tells you.
- **Tony Reinke — tonyreinke.com.** Tagline: *"Miscellanies from the Sonoran Desert"* (deliberately oblique). Nav items name his books (*Lit!, Newton, Smartphones, Spectacles, The Joy Project*) — Christian content readers recognize, others don't until they click.

**Why it works:** Lowest-friction entry for mixed audiences. Christian visitors decode the signals quickly; non-Christian visitors aren't repelled.

**Why it can fail:** Under-signals to the Christian audience that the creator wants to reach. If the explicit goal is "I want visitors to know I'm Christian within seconds," this strategy fails the brief.

**Transferability:** Weak match for Fellipe's explicit ask. He wants the signal *visible* in seconds, not buried in nav.

---

### Strategy 5 — Tagged-Content Split (one site, blog tags do the work)
Single domain, single hero. Faith content lives in a tagged blog category; professional content lives in another. The hero is professional; the content stream is mixed.

**Examples:**
- **Tim Challies — challies.com** is the inverse extreme (everything is faith content), but the *mechanism* — tags + categories carrying the identity — is the pattern. Tagline: *"Informing the Reforming Daily Since 2003"* signals to insiders ("Reforming" = Reformed theology) without using "Christian" outright.
- **Jon Acuff — jonacuff.com.** Hero: *"Turning Starters into Finishers by Teaching People How to Think."* Title: *"New York Times Bestselling Author and INC Top 100 Leadership Speaker."* No faith above the fold *at all* despite his earlier *Stuff Christians Like* blog. He keeps faith content on a separate property.

**Why it works:** Lets a creator publish to two audiences without forcing either to wade through the other.

**Why it can fail:** Can read as compartmentalized — "I'll only mention faith when it's safe." Risk of appearing inauthentic to the Christian reader.

**Transferability:** Partial. Useful for blog architecture (tag faith essays distinctly), but not the right *hero* strategy for someone whose explicit ask is "I want my faith visible up front."

---

### Strategy 6 — Two-Site Split (hard separation: professional domain ≠ personal/faith domain)
The most extreme version of separation. The professional site doesn't mention faith at all. A second site (or different platform — Substack, podcast, second domain) carries the faith material. Cross-link is subtle.

**Examples:**
- **Patrick Lencioni** runs **tablegroup.com** as his consulting firm — hero: *"Successful companies. Cohesive teams. Engaged employees."* Zero mention of Catholicism. His faith work is on **The Amazing Parish** (separate org) and through Catholic Answers / podcasts. The fence is real.
- **Skye Jethani's** older personal domain redirects to **withgoddaily.com**, his devotional product — but his podcast work (*Holy Post*) is its own platform. Faith identity has multiple homes, none of them on a generic personal site.

**Why it works:** Maximum optionality for mixed audiences. Buyers of professional services never see the faith content unless they look.

**Why it can fail:** Reads as hiding. Fellipe's stated brief ("won't soften or hide his faith") explicitly rules this out.

**Transferability:** Not a match. Listed for completeness.

## B. Anti-patterns (5)

1. **Christianese in the hero.** Phrases like *"To the glory of God alone,"* *"For His Kingdom,"* *"Soli Deo Gloria"* in the masthead. Insiders nod; outsiders bounce. Search-result evidence: church-website style guides repeatedly flag *"Christian jargon"* / *"Christianese"* as the #1 alienator on faith-based sites — *"insider church lingo doesn't connect with most people, especially those who grew up outside the church bubble"* (OurChurch web-design guide). The same logic applies to a personal brand site for mixed-faith clients.

2. **Verse-as-decoration without context.** A Bible reference (e.g., *"Colossians 3:23"*) plastered in the hero or footer with no surrounding meaning. Either the visitor knows the verse (insiders only) or they don't (and it reads as a random code). Anti-pattern because it requires audience pre-qualification to land.

3. **Bait-and-switch hero.** Generic professional hero, then 200 words into the About page a sudden hard pivot to evangelism / salvation invitation. Reads as deceptive. Better to disclose earlier and softer than to ambush.

4. **Faith as the *only* differentiator in a B2B context.** Hero like *"Christian software developer"* or *"Faith-driven web design"* — these exist (himfirstmedia.com, christian-webdesigner.com, faithwebdesigners.com all use this pattern) and they self-select to a Christian-only client base by design. For a creator who explicitly wants mixed-faith clients (Fellipe's stated brief), this anti-pattern collapses the addressable market on purpose. Fine if that's the strategy; fatal if it's not.

5. **Devotional-product positioning on a personal brand site.** *"The devotional for people who hate devotionals"* (Skye Jethani) works because Jethani's primary product *is* the devotional. If Fellipe leads with devotional/spiritual-product language but his actual offering is software/consulting, the hero misrepresents the business. Match hero genre to actual product.

## C. Christian-tech-leader case studies (5)

### Jordan Raynor — jordanraynor.com
- **Role:** Author, devotional creator, Executive Chairman of Threshold 360 (venture-backed startup), former White House staffer (Bush admin), Google Fellow.
- **Hero copy verbatim:** *"Your work matters for eternity."*
- **Where faith shows up:** Hero (overt — "eternity"), nav ("Devotionals"), CTA ("Join 100,000+ Christians who receive The Word Before Work"), about page opener ("a leading voice of the faith and work movement"), book titles (*The Sacredness of Secular Work*, *Five Mere Christians*).
- **What works:** Total clarity. No one is confused about who this site is for. Audience self-selects in 2 seconds.
- **What works less well:** Hard filter against non-Christian clients. If a tech executive wanted to hire him for a non-faith venture, the site signals "not for you." Raynor accepts that tradeoff because his market *is* the Christian-business segment.

### John Dyer — j.hn
- **Role:** Coder + professor at Dallas Theological Seminary; author on theology and tech.
- **Hero copy verbatim:** *"Coder and Professor, Technology and Faith."* Subtitle: *"Theology, Technology, Society."*
- **Where faith shows up:** Hero (named explicitly as one of two domains), recent post titles (*"9 Bible Verses on Artificial Intelligence,"* *"The Incarnation and the Gender of God"*), credentials ("PhD," DTS).
- **What works:** Compound identity. "Coder *and* Professor, Technology *and* Faith" — the conjunction does the heavy lifting. Reads as a person, not a pitch.
- **What works less well:** Audience is narrow by design (theology-meets-tech academics). Less applicable to a founder selling to general business clients.

### Tony Reinke — tonyreinke.com
- **Role:** Author at Desiring God, theologian, technology critic.
- **Hero copy verbatim:** *"Tony Reinke | Miscellanies from the Sonoran Desert."*
- **Where faith shows up:** Tagline is deliberately oblique; faith surfaces through nav (book names like *Spectacles* — about screens — and *The Joy Project* — about Christian Hedonism), categories (*God, Christ, Theology, Reformed theology*), and content.
- **What works:** Literary, restrained, draws Christian readers without alienating curious outsiders.
- **What works less well:** Doesn't telegraph "Christian" within seconds. Fails the Fellipe brief specifically.

### Andy Crouch — andy-crouch.com
- **Role:** Author (*The Tech-Wise Family, Culture Making, Strong and Weak, Playing God*), partner for theology and culture at Praxis, teaching fellow at Practicing the Way.
- **Hero copy verbatim:** *"Andy Crouch"* / *"Author, musician, public speaker."*
- **Where faith shows up:** Book copy (*The Life We're Looking For* — relational/spiritual framing), bio ("partner for theology and culture"), book themes (Mammon, image-bearing, redemptive entrepreneurship).
- **What works:** Hero is dignified, professional, role-based. Theological depth lives in the work itself. A non-Christian buyer of *The Tech-Wise Family* never feels preached at; a Christian reader recognizes the framework instantly.
- **What works less well:** Visitor doesn't know he's Christian until they click into a book. May fail the "within seconds" bar Fellipe set.

### Russell Moore — russellmoore.com
- **Role:** Editor-in-chief of Christianity Today, former president of the Southern Baptist Ethics & Religious Liberty Commission, author.
- **Hero copy verbatim:** *"Engaging the Culture without Losing the Gospel."*
- **Where faith shows up:** Tagline (overt — "Gospel"), nav (Newsletter, Books, *The Russell Moore Show*), featured content (always faith-engaged).
- **What works:** Tagline doubles as a *positioning statement* — it tells you both *what* he does and *whose side he's on* in one line. The verb "Engaging" signals openness; "Without Losing" signals conviction. That balance is the whole brief in 7 words.
- **What works less well:** Audience is presumed evangelical-adjacent. Less useful for a founder who needs to sell software to a Buddhist VP of Engineering.

## D. Above-the-fold treatment for Christian-first

**The strongest evidence-backed approach for a mixed-faith audience: a *single sentence* that names faith plainly and pairs it with a professional identity in the same breath.**

The Russell Moore tagline (*"Engaging the Culture without Losing the Gospel"*) and the John Dyer hero (*"Coder and Professor, Technology and Faith"*) are the clearest instances. Both name the religious frame *and* the professional frame in one line. Neither hides; neither preaches.

**What to avoid above the fold (anti-evidence):**

- **Verses without context** (anti-pattern 2). No site reviewed used a Bible verse as the hero. Even Tim Challies — whose audience is 100% Christian — uses *"Informing the Reforming Daily Since 2003,"* not a verse.
- **"For the glory of God" / "Soli Deo Gloria" in the masthead** (anti-pattern 1). No site reviewed used Latin theological phrases above the fold for a personal brand. The phrase appears in publishing imprints (Soli Deo Gloria Publishing) but not on personal author/founder sites — likely because it requires insider parsing.
- **A salvation invitation or prayer** in the hero. No reviewed site does this. The closest is Jordan Raynor's "your work matters for eternity," and even that is positioned as a value claim about *work*, not a soteriological invitation.

**Tradeoffs in single-line treatments:**

| Treatment | Strength | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Theological noun in hero** ("Gospel," "eternity," "Kingdom") | Maximum clarity for Christian audience | Filters secular clients |
| **Values vocabulary that codes as Christian** ("calling," "grace," "stewardship," "purpose") | Mixed-audience friendly | Christian audience may not recognize fast enough |
| **Identity stack** ("Christian, founder, builder...") | Honest, polymath, hard to argue with | Loses single-claim punch |
| **Compound conjunction** ("X and Faith," "X without losing Y") | Reads as integrated, not bolted-on | Requires precise wording — easy to get clunky |

**The compound-conjunction pattern (Dyer / Moore) is the strongest evidence for Fellipe's stated constraint** — visible Christian identity within seconds, without filtering out non-Christian buyers — because the Christian word is present *and* the professional word is present *and* the grammatical structure makes them equal partners, not subordinates.

## Recommendations specific to Fellipe

1. **Hero pattern: compound conjunction.** Adapt the Dyer/Moore structure. Examples to test against the brand: *"Christian. Entrepreneur. Builder of tools that last."* / *"Founder, builder, follower of Jesus — building software with intent."* / *"Bossa Nova Solutions — Christian-led, client-focused, built for the long game."* The conjunction (and / period / em-dash) does the work of integrating faith with profession on equal footing — visible in seconds, not preachy. Evidence: Dyer's *"Coder and Professor, Technology and Faith"* and Moore's *"Engaging the Culture without Losing the Gospel"* are the two cleanest live examples.

2. **Identity stack on the About page, not the hero.** DHH-style multi-identity ("Christian, entrepreneur, founder, husband, builder, writer") works powerfully *one click deep* and lets visitors who want detail get it without forcing it on every page. Keeps the hero punchy while the about page does the layering.

3. **Avoid all three primary anti-patterns in the hero: no verses, no Christianese, no Latin theological phrases.** Save those for the writing/blog (Strategy 5 — tagged content). Reinke and Challies prove that a serious Christian audience finds the work via *the work*, not via a hero that pre-screens them. Hero earns the click; the writing earns the alignment.
