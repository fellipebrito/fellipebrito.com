# R1 — Pattern Scan

- Date: 2026-04-27
- Sites surveyed: 12
- Method: WebFetch + WebSearch. Hero copy and nav labels quoted verbatim where rendered. Where a site is JS-heavy or returned a thin shell, that's noted in the "what doesn't" or method line for the entry.
- Cap used: ~10 WebFetch calls.

Categories represented:
- Christian-positioned tech/business creators: Jordan Raynor, John Dyer, Tony Reinke, Andy Crouch, Tim Challies (5)
- Tech founders / operators: Patrick Collison, DHH, Pieter Levels, Naval Ravikant, Sahil Lavingia (5)
- Developer-writers: Lee Robinson, Wes Bos, Tania Rascia, Julian Lehr (4)
- Multi-track: Austin Kleon, Sahil Lavingia, DHH (counted in their primary categories above; they are also the multi-track exemplars)

Skye Jethani's root domain (skyejethani.com) 301-redirects to withgoddaily.com — a devotional product, not a personal-brand site — so he was dropped and Tim Challies + Austin Kleon took the slot.

## Site 1: Jordan Raynor (jordanraynor.com)

- **Owner:** Christian author/podcaster on the theology of work; bestselling business/faith books.
- **Hero (verbatim):** "Your work matters for eternity." — sub: "I'm here to help you see how and what to do about it."
- **Sub-tagline / promo strip:** "Redeeming Your Goals is available for pre-order!" with positioning around "seven biblical principles and 58 practices."
- **Portrait:** No portrait above the fold. Logo + book promo image dominate the hero.
- **Nav (verbatim):** Devotionals, Podcast, Books, Courses, Speaking, About, Contact, Login.
- **Identity signaling:** The hero sentence collapses theology + ambition into one promise ("work matters for eternity"). Within ten seconds you know: he's Christian, he writes about work, he's selling something. The nav reinforces it (Devotionals + Speaking + Courses).
- **Project/work showcase:** Single big book hero. No portfolio of past clients. Books and courses are surfaced via dedicated nav items, not laid out on the homepage.
- **Writing surface:** Newsletter is the conversion surface, not the blog. "Join 100,000+ Christians who receive The Word Before Work devotionals every Monday!" — exact subscriber count is the social proof.
- **Contact / CTA:** Email-capture form is the primary CTA. Pre-order button is the secondary.
- **What works:** Hero sentence is doing real work — it's both a worldview claim AND a value proposition. "Your work matters for eternity" is faith-forward without being preachy, and it qualifies the audience instantly.
- **What doesn't:** The login nav item leaks the SaaS/course back-end into the public face. Slightly muddies what kind of site this is (creator? platform?).

## Site 2: John Dyer (j.hn)

- **Owner:** Coder + seminary professor; writes on theology and technology.
- **Hero (verbatim):** "John Dyer" / "Theology, Technology, Society"
- **Sub-tagline:** "Coder and Professor, Technology and Faith"
- **Portrait:** Yes — professional headshot beneath the hero.
- **Nav (verbatim):** About, Publications, Projects, Speaking, Contact, Theology Technology and Digital Culture (Fall 2023 Course), RSS
- **Identity signaling:** Three-word triad ("Theology, Technology, Society") plus the headshot does the work. The role descriptor ("Coder and Professor") prevents ambiguity. You know in five seconds.
- **Project/work showcase:** Dedicated "Projects" route, not on home. Home is dominated by recent articles in a card grid.
- **Writing surface:** Above-the-fold-ish — cards of latest 8 posts with thumbnails, byline, date, excerpt. Blog IS the homepage.
- **Contact / CTA:** Plain Contact nav link. RSS exposed in nav (rare and on-brand for an academic).
- **What works:** Triad tagline. Three nouns is enough to position someone with multiple intersecting interests, and it scans cleanly.
- **What doesn't:** A dated course link in primary nav ("Fall 2023") makes the site feel un-tended in 2026. Stale nav items are an active credibility leak.

## Site 3: Tony Reinke (tonyreinke.com)

- **Owner:** Christian author on tech, attention, and spirituality (Desiring God orbit).
- **Hero (verbatim):** "Tony Reinke | Miscellanies from the Sonoran Desert"
- **Sub-tagline:** None. Goes straight into article stream.
- **Portrait:** No persistent portrait. Article featured images dominate.
- **Nav (verbatim):** Home, About Tony Reinke, Lit!, Newton, Smartphones, Spectacles, The Joy Project
- **Identity signaling:** Weakest of the Christian set. Tagline ("Miscellanies from the Sonoran Desert") tells you he's contemplative and lives in Arizona but doesn't tell you what he does. You have to click in.
- **Project/work showcase:** Books are sidebar widgets — covers + Amazon links. Books are also nav items (each title gets its own route).
- **Writing surface:** Reverse-chronological blog IS the homepage; full excerpts.
- **Contact / CTA:** Email subscribe ("Join 1,713 other subscribers") — small number, possibly counter-intuitively credible-feeling.
- **What works:** Each major book becomes a nav route. For an author with a defined backlist, this is a clean way to make the catalog navigable without a separate "Books" landing.
- **What doesn't:** No identity signal in the hero. A new visitor can't tell within ten seconds whether he's a journalist, pastor, novelist, or hobbyist.

## Site 4: Andy Crouch (andy-crouch.com)

- **Owner:** Christian author/speaker on culture, technology, faith, and the family.
- **Hero (verbatim):** "Andy Crouch" / "Author, musician, public speaker"
- **Sub-tagline:** None — page leads into book sections.
- **Portrait:** Yes (referenced; image present).
- **Nav (verbatim):** The Life, Tech-Wise, Strong and Weak, Playing God, Culture Making, Articles, Bio
- **Identity signaling:** Three-noun tagline ("Author, musician, public speaker") + immediate book wall. Faith-forward via subject matter, not via overt religious copy.

- **What doesn't:** No newsletter capture. For a 2026 creator site, leaving the email channel on the table is a missed conversion surface.

## Site 5: Tim Challies (challies.com)

- **Owner:** Reformed Christian blogger; daily output for 20+ years.
- **Hero (verbatim):** "Informing the Reforming Daily Since 2003"
- **Sub-tagline (verbatim):** "Welcome. Every piece of content on this site has been handwritten or hand-selected for you without any use of AI. Enjoy this truly human experience!"
- **Portrait:** Small Gravatar in author footer, not above the fold.
- **Nav (verbatim):** Explore, About, Subscribe, Daily Kindle Deals, Book Recommendations, Best Commentaries, My Books, Become a Patron, Advertise, Contact
- **Identity signaling:** Tagline pun ("Informing the Reforming") immediately flags Reformed Protestant. "Since 2003" is the credibility anchor.
- **Project/work showcase:** "My Books" nav item with 10 titles; book covers throughout. Author identity is layered behind the daily-content identity.
- **Writing surface:** Daily article feed IS the site. Articles, Book Reviews, "A La Carte" link roundups, trending section.
- **Contact / CTA:** Email signup form embedded; Patreon (creator-economy monetization); standard social row.
- **What works:** The "no AI" sub-tagline is a 2026 differentiator that other creator sites haven't caught up to. As LLM slop floods the web, "handwritten" reads as a quality signal.
- **What doesn't:** Nav is bloated (10 items including Advertise, Daily Kindle Deals, Become a Patron). Primary identity gets diluted by monetization clutter.

## Site 6: Patrick Collison (patrickcollison.com)

- **Owner:** CEO of Stripe.
- **Hero (verbatim):** "Patrick Collison" — no tagline, no role description.
- **Sub-tagline:** None.
- **Portrait:** None.
- **Nav (verbatim):** About, Advice, Blog, Bookshelf, Culture, Enlightenment, Fast, Growth, Labs, Links, Novels, People, Pollution, Progress, Questions, Solar, SV history, Travel
- **Identity signaling:** None on the homepage. The site assumes you already know who he is. Identity is built laterally through the catalog of essays/lists (Fast, Progress, Growth — these read as a manifesto by aggregation).
- **Project/work showcase:** None on home. Stripe gets no logo treatment. The site is content-only.
- **Writing surface:** Each topic is its own nav item (a "Bookshelf" page, a "Fast" page, etc.). Topical structure rather than chronological blog.
- **Contact / CTA:** None visible.
- **What works:** When you're already famous in your domain, removing the hero entirely and going straight to the substance reads as confident, not lazy. The topic-as-page structure (rather than chronological blog) gives essays a much longer shelf life.
- **What doesn't:** Completely unusable for someone discovering him cold. There's no entry point if you don't already know "this is the Stripe guy." Replicating this without his fame would be self-defeating.

## Site 7: David Heinemeier Hansson (dhh.dk)

- **Owner:** Creator of Ruby on Rails; co-owner of 37signals; race-car driver.
- **Hero (verbatim):** "DAVID HEINEMEIER HANSSON" / "I am the creator of Ruby on Rails, creator of Omarchy, co-owner of 37signals..."
- **Sub-tagline:** Inline links to HEY World blog, REWORK podcast, social platforms, email.
- **Portrait:** Yes — square headshot.
- **Nav (verbatim, anchored sections):** Rails, Omarchy, 37signals, Rework, Driver, Investor, Board, Podcasts, Family
- **Identity signaling:** Brutally explicit. The hero is a comma-separated identity declaration ("I am the creator of X, creator of Y, co-owner of Z..."). No mystery, no minimalism flex.
- **Project/work showcase:** Each major track of his career = a nav anchor. "Driver" sits alongside "Rails" — same hierarchy. This is how you signal multi-track without apology.
- **Writing surface:** Writing offloaded to HEY World (his own platform); the personal site only points there.
- **Contact / CTA:** Public email (`dhh@hey.com`) + social row.
- **What works:** "I am the X of Y, the X of Y, the X of Y" hero formula — radical, stackable, and resilient. As his career adds tracks (racing, OS distro, podcast), the hero just absorbs them. No taxonomy decisions required.
- **What doesn't:** Personal-life sections ("Family", "Driver") sit at the same nav weight as professional ones. For a client-acquisition site this would be wrong; for a famous-founder personal site it's a flex that signals "you don't get to compartmentalize me."

- **Sub-tagline:** Bio paragraph mentioning Nomad List, Remote OK, MAKE book, advocacy for remote work.
- **Portrait:** Not surfaced in the rendered shell.

- **Identity signaling:** Hero is a story-as-positioning sentence. One line and you know the entire arc. Origin story as tagline.
- **Project/work showcase:** Linked inline within the bio paragraph. No grid, no logos. You hunt for them.
- **Writing surface:** Reverse-chron blog (2011–2022), explicit note that he's stopped blogging here and now publishes on X.

## Cross-cutting takeaways

### Patterns that work

1. **Hero = role + mission in two sentences.** Lee Robinson's "I'm a developer and writer. I work at Cursor teaching about AI." + "My life's work is to make technology easy to understand and interesting to learn about." is the cleanest version. Wes Bos's "Hey, I'm Wes Bos. I'm here to help you become a really good web developer." is the warm version. The pattern: sentence 1 anchors who, sentence 2 anchors why anyone should care.

2. **Three-noun tagline for multi-track identity.** "Founder, Writer, Painter" (Sahil), "Author, musician, public speaker" (Crouch), "software engineer, open-source creator, and former professional chef" (Rascia). Three is the magic number — two undersells, four feels unfocused. Order matters: lead with the highest-status track for the primary audience.

3. **Books / projects as their own nav routes.** Andy Crouch and Tony Reinke both promote individual book titles to top-level nav. For a creator with a defined catalog, this beats a generic "Books" page because each title gets its own SEO surface and the nav itself becomes a credibility list.

4. **Newsletter subscriber count as social proof.** Jordan Raynor: "Join 100,000+ Christians..." Tony Reinke: "Join 1,713 other subscribers." Both work — large numbers signal scale, small numbers signal intimacy. The pattern: don't ask for an email without giving a reason.

5. **Curated featured-writing instead of full archive on home.** Lee Robinson's "Some of my favorite writing includes" with five hand-picked links beats a chronological wall of recent posts. Curation tells the visitor what you want them to read; chronology tells them you wrote a lot.

6. **Faith / worldview as a nav item, not a homepage statement.** Lee Robinson has "beliefs" as a top-level nav slug. Andy Crouch's faith is implicit in his book topics, never declared on the home. The pattern: signal the worldview through the work and through one explicit page, not via a banner.

7. **Origin story as hero copy.** Pieter Levels's "A few years ago I sold all my stuff..." is more memorable than any descriptor. Story beats label when the story is short enough to fit in a single sentence.

8. **Public email beats contact form.** DHH (`dhh@hey.com`), Andy Crouch (`andy at praxis dot co`), Lee Robinson (`lee@leerob.com`). For a personal-brand site, a real address signals reachability and confidence. Forms feel corporate.

### Anti-patterns

1. **Bloated nav that mixes monetization with identity.** Tim Challies's nav has 10 items including "Advertise," "Daily Kindle Deals," "Become a Patron." The result: the primary identity is diluted by sales clutter. Five-to-seven nav items is the sweet spot.

2. **Stale time-stamped nav items.** John Dyer's "Theology Technology and Digital Culture (Fall 2023 Course)" in primary nav in 2026 actively damages credibility. Anything with a year or term in nav must have an owner who removes it on schedule.

3. **Mononym/no-tagline hero when you're not already famous.** Patrick Collison and Naval can pull off a name-only hero because the audience already knows them. For everyone else, this reads as either lazy or assumes-too-much. Don't copy a famous person's hero unless you also have their existing recognition.

4. **"I no longer post here" inside a still-public blog.** Pieter Levels's note that he's stopped blogging on his own site, inside his blog, leaks "this site isn't tended." Either redirect, archive cleanly with a frozen note in the about page, or keep posting.

5. **No portrait on a personal-trust site.** Wes Bos and Lee Robinson both rely entirely on logos/text. For a personal brand whose business model IS parasocial trust (especially client work, where the client is hiring a person), a missing face is a missed signal. The Christian set is more disciplined here — Dyer, Crouch, Challies all surface a face.

### Christian-first + dual-audience-specific patterns

1. **Faith comes through the value claim, not the banner.** Jordan Raynor's "Your work matters for eternity" is theology compressed into a value proposition — it qualifies the audience (Christians or the faith-curious) without ever using the word Christian. Compare Tim Challies's "Informing the Reforming" — also faith-forward but assumes the visitor already speaks the dialect. For a dual-audience site (clients + readers), Raynor's pattern is more transferable: faith-forward enough that the right audience self-selects, plain-speech enough that a non-Christian potential client isn't bounced.

2. **Two-track audiences need two CTAs, not one.** Crouch and Raynor both let speaking inquiries (clients) and newsletter signups (readers) live side-by-side without merging them. Lee Robinson does the same with `lee@leerob.com` (work) and the writing archive (readers). The anti-pattern would be forcing every visitor through a single funnel — clients want a calendar/email, readers want a feed. Give both.

3. **Beliefs page > beliefs banner.** Lee Robinson's `/beliefs` route is the model worth stealing for the Christian-first goal. The homepage doesn't lead with theology; it leads with role + mission. But theology gets its own dedicated, indexable, link-shareable page. This lets the right audience find the worldview deliberately, without making every cold business-development visitor click through a sermon to find the work.
